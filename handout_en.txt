Mood Music
==========

Mood Music is a generative music installation. A camera mounted above a
piano keyboard reads the facial expression of anyone who sits down, and
uses that reading to shape an improvised piano melody in real time. The
moment the camera detects a face, the music begins. When the visitor
stands up, it stops.

The system recognises eight emotional states: happiness, sadness, anger,
fear, surprise, disgust, contempt, and neutrality. Each maps to a
distinct musical character. Happiness produces bright, dense melodic
lines in a major scale at a lively tempo. Sadness slows the music,
drops it into a lower register, and leans on the minor scale and
expressive blue notes. Anger fills the texture with fast chromatic runs
and heavy dissonance. Fear generates sparse, unpredictable phrases with
wide dynamic swings. Surprise throws the melody across a wide register
with erratic leaps. Disgust produces grinding, reluctant lines heavy
with tension. Contempt is cold and minimal — a few dry, detached notes.
Neutrality settles in the middle: moderate tempo, moderate density,
mostly consonant.

Underneath the melody, the accompaniment follows one of three jazz chord
progressions — a twelve-bar blues, thirty-two-bar Rhythm Changes (the
harmonic basis of many bebop standards), or the Coltrane Changes from
Giant Steps — cycling through each in turn.

How the work was made
---------------------

Mood Music was built through an iterative conversation with an AI coding
assistant. No code was written by hand. The work was shaped entirely
through high-level instructions: describing what the music should feel
like for each emotion, asking for a chord progression to be added,
specifying how the camera feed should be handled, and so on. Each
exchange produced a working version of the program, which was then
tested, heard, and refined through further instructions. The assistant
also wrote the accompanying documentation — and this text — in the same
way: by describing purpose and content in plain language and editing the
result through conversation.

The process produced not only a working program but a detailed log of
every design decision, recorded as the work evolved. That log, along
with the full source code, is available at:

    https://github.com/muggezifter/mood_music
